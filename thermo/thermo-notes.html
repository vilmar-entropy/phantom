1.1 Introduction
Thermodynamics involves the storage, transformation, and transfer of energy. Energy is stored as internal energy (due to temperature), kinetic energy (due to motion), potential energy (due to elevation), and chemical energy (due to chemical composition); it is transformed from one of these forms to another; and it is transferred across a boundary as either heat or work.
1.2 The System and Control Volume
A thermodynamic system is a fixed quantity of matter upon which attention is focused. It is a region enclosed by a specified boundaries , which may be imaginary, either fixed or moving. All matter and space external to a system is its surroundings . Systems may be defined in several ways; for present purposes, we wish to distinguish between three kinds. A closed system is one in which there is no exchange of matter with the surroundings - mass does not cross its boundaries. An open system is one across whose boundaries there is a flow of mass. Each may have energy crossing its boundary. An isolated system is one that is completely impervious to its surroundings - neither mass nor energy cross its boundaries. In Figure 1.1 let the compressed gas be the system and the movable piston be its boundary. If heat be applied externally to the cylinder, the gas will undergo an increase in temperature, raising the piston. With the rise of piston, the boundary moves. Energy (heat and work) crosses the boundary during this process while the mass of gaseous matter remains constant within the system.
Often the system under analysis is of open type, as a pump shown in Fig 1.2. With open systems we normally specify the boundary as a control surface and the volume encompassed by that surface as the control volume . Thus the control volume may be defined as a volume in space in which one has interest for a particular study or analysis. The mass of working substance within the volume may be constant (though not the same molecular matter at any given instant) as in the case of pump, or it may be varying as in the case of tire undergoing inflation.
1.3 Properties and State of a System
A property is any quantity which serves to describe a system. The state of a system is its condition as described by giving values to its properties at a particular instant.
Properties may be classified as intensive or extensive. Intensive properties are independent of the mass; for example, temperature, pressure, density, and the voltage. Extensive properties are dependent upon the mass of the system and are total values such as total volume and total internal energy.
If we divide an extensive property by the mass, a specific property results. Specific properties are those for a unit mass and are intensive by definition such as specific volume.

1.4 Mass and Weight
The mass of a body is the absolute quantity of matter in it. The weight of a body means the force of gravity $F_g$ on the body. $$F_g = mg \longrightarrow (1.1)$$
1.5 Density, Specific Volume, Specific Weight
The density $\rho$ of any substance is its mass (not weight) per unit volume. $$\rho = \frac{m}{V} \longrightarrow (1.2)$$ The specific volume v is the volume of a unit mass and is the reciprocal of the density. $$ \text{v} = \frac{V}{m} = \frac{1}{\rho} \longrightarrow (1.3)$$ The specific weight $\gamma$ of any substance is the force of gravity on unit volume. $$\gamma = \frac{F_g}{V} \longrightarrow (1.4)$$
1.6 Pressure
In gases and liquids it is common to call the effect of a normal force acting on an area the pressure. The SI unit of pressure is the Pascal (Pa) where 1 Pa = 1 $N/m^2 = 1 kg/{m \cdot s^2} $. The corresponding English unit is $lb_f/ft^2$, although $lb_f/in^2$ (psi) is commonly used. In the atmosphere pressure varies with elevation. This variation can be expressed mathematically by $$ P = \gamma h = \rho gh \longrightarrow (1.5) $$ The standard reference atmospheric pressure is 760 mmHg or 29.92 inHg at 32$^{\circ}$F, or 14.7 psia, or 1 atm, or 101.325 kPa. In most thermodynamic relations absolute pressure must be used. Absolute pressure is gage pressure plus the local atmospheric prssure: $$ P_{abs} = P_{atm} + P_g \longrightarrow (1.6)$$ A negative gage pressure is often called a vacuum, and gages capable of reading negative pressures are vacuum gages. A gage pressure of -50 kPa would be referred to as a vacuum of 50kPa, with the sign omitted. Fig 1.8 shows the relationships between absolute and gage pressure.
1.7 Temperature
As Maxwell said, the temperature of a body is its thermal state considered with reference to its ability to communicate heat to other bodies. It is an intensive property that is a measure of the intensity of the stored molecular energy in a system. Zeroth Law states that when two bodies, isolated from other environment, are in thermal equilibrium with a third body, the two are in thermal equilibrium with each other. The Fahrenheit and Celsius temperature scales are related as follows: $$ t_F = \frac{9}{5}t_C + 32 \longrightarrow (1.7) $$ $$ t_C = \frac{5}{9} (t_F - 32) \longrightarrow (1.8) $$ Absolute temperature is the temperature measured from absolute zero. Absolute zero temperature is the temperature t which all molecular motion ceases. Degrees Fahrenheit $ (^{\circ}F) $ and degrees Celsius $ (^{\circ}C) $ indicate temperature reading (t). Fahrenheit degrees $ (F^{\circ}) $ and Celsius degrees $ (C^{\circ})$ indicate temperature change or difference. $$ 1.8F ^{\circ} = 1 C ^{\circ} $$ It follows that, $$ 1 F ^{\circ} = 1 R ^{\circ} $$ and $$ C ^{\circ} = 1 K ^{\circ} $$ The relations between absolute and relative temperatures are $$ T_R = t_F + 460 \longrightarrow (1.9) $$ $$ T_K = t_C + 273 \longrightarrow (1.10) $$
1.8 Conservation of Mass
The law of conservation of mass states that mass in indestructible. The amount of fluid passing through a given section assuming its density is the same at all points of the cross section of area A is $$ \dot m = \rho v A \longrightarrow (1.11)$$ Accepting the statement of the law of conservation of mass, it follows with respect to any system, that the verbal form of the law is
$[\text{mass entering}] - $ $ [\text{mass leaving}] = $ $[\text{change of mass stored in system }] $ $ \longrightarrow (1.12)$
A steady-flow system is an open system in which there is no change of stored mass.
$[\text{mass entering}] = $ $ [\text{mass leaving}] $
$ \dot m = \rho _1 A_1 v_1 = \rho _2 A_2 v_2 \longrightarrow (1.13)$
The resulting equation $(1.13)$ is called continuity equation of steady flow.
